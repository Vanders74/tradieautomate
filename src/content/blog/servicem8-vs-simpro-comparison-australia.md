## Ease of Use: Mobile-First vs Feature-Heavy

This is where the platforms diverge most dramatically.

### ServiceM8: Built for the Field

ServiceM8 was designed with a mobile-first philosophy. The iOS and Android apps are genuinely good — not an afterthought. A solar installer can open a job, pull up compliance checklists, capture photos, collect a customer signature, and send an invoice from the roof without switching apps.

The interface is clean and intuitive. Most new users can navigate the basics within a day. For trade businesses where technicians aren't office workers, this matters enormously.

### simPRO: Powerful but Complex

simPRO's strength is its depth. You can model complex multi-stage projects, track materials across warehouses, manage subcontractors, and run sophisticated project accounting. But that depth comes with a learning curve.

New users typically need formal training. The mobile app is functional but less polished than ServiceM8. For office managers running complex commercial projects, the power is worth it. For a solar installer trying to run clean residential jobs efficiently, it's often overkill.

**Verdict:** ServiceM8 wins on usability, especially for field-based teams.

## Mobile-First vs Desktop-First

This distinction matters more than most software comparisons acknowledge.

**ServiceM8** is genuinely mobile-first. The app is where work happens — from dispatch to invoicing. The web interface exists and is useful for scheduling and reporting, but the product was built around field use.

**simPRO** is desktop-first. The web interface is where most of the power lives. The mobile app is a companion tool for field staff, not the primary interface. For office-based project managers running complex jobs, this is fine. For sole traders or small teams where the owner is on-site daily, it creates friction.
